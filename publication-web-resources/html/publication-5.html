Menstruation is a natural part of the reproductive cycle, yet across cultures it has long been treated as a source of stigma, silence and restriction. Historically, menstruating individuals have been excluded from daily activities and spaces, with beliefs and practices shaping behaviour in both private and public life. While global conversations now increasingly frame menstruation through equality, autonomy and gender inclusivity, many traditions continue to influence how people move, interact and identify.

In India, menstruation remains deeply ritualised and tied to cultural and religious customs that regulate access to rooms, objects, food and social interaction. Growing up in this environment, I experienced the tension between inherited expectations and personal values of agency and feminist thought.

This dissertation investigates the origins and persistence of such practices, how they shape domestic and urban spaces, and how rethinking them could lead to more inclusive, supportive public environments for all who menstruate.
